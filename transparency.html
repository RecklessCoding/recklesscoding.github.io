Transparency in AI

"Robots are manufactured artefacts. They should not be designed in a deceptive way to exploit vulnerable users; instead their machine nature should be transparent." - EPSRC Principles of Robotics

What is transparency?

We defined transparency as having meaningful information of a system's status at any specific moment or period of time (Theodorou et al., 2017). In layman's terms, the direct workings of the system should be made apparent—visible and comprehensible. Transparency is, therefore, the property of a system whereby it is possible to trace responsibility and, if necessary, assign accountability (Bryson and Theodorou, 2017). Moreover, where there is transparency, there does not need to be blind trust (or distrust) to the system. The user is informed of the status—and limitations—of the system, enabling calibration of trust.

In later works, we expanded our definition to include: 1) having access, even if so in the form of code or a static manual, to general principles by which decisions are made ; and 2) any physical cues in case of embodied systems (Theodorou, 2019). Our high-level definition is designed such as to allow context-specific implementations of transparency; i.e. accommodate a range of systems, stakeholders, and socio-legal factors.

Designing User-centric Transparent Systems

First, it needs to be clear: there is no 'magic' one-size-fits-all solution (Theodorou et al., 2017). Different stakeholders have distinct needs; information relevant for one user may be irrelevant, or even harmful, for another.
